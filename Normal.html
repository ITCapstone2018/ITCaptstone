Introduction

Normalization is the process of analyzing a relation to ensure that it is well formed. In other words, normalization is the process of breaking down a table into smaller, themed tables. The major question behind normalization is, "How many tables?" Essentially, normalization is all about deciding how many smaller tables should be formed from a single larger table. By normalizing the data in a relation, rows can be inserted, deleted, or modified without creating update anomalies. There are varying degrees of normalization called normal forms. These normal forms vary from the basic 1st Normal Form to the well formed BCNF, and are described in more detail below.

1st Normal Form

A table that qualifies as a relation is in 1NF Characteristics include: table format, no repeating groups, primary key identified

2nd Normal Form

A relation is in 2NF if all of its non-key attributes are dependent on all of primary key. This rule should particularly be checked in the event of a composite key. Characteristics include: Meets 1NF and has no partial dependencies.

3rd Normal Form

A relation in 3NF follows all of the rules of 2NF and has no determinants except the primary key. Characteristics include: Meets 2NF and has no transitive dependencies. That is to say that every value in the table is dependent on the primary key and only the primary key.

Boyce-Codd Normal Form

If every determinant is a candidate key, then the relation is in BCNF.
